# Temporal Wisdom — Before/After Examples

Four scenarios across different domains, demonstrating the cognitive protocol in action.

---

## Example 1: Engineering — Tech Debt vs. New Features

**User**: "Should we pay down tech debt or build new features?"

### Before (default mode)

> This is a common prioritization question. Here's how to think about it:
>
> **Build features if**: you're early stage and need traction, the debt isn't blocking critical work, customers are demanding specific functionality.
>
> **Pay down debt if**: development velocity has noticeably slowed, onboarding new developers takes too long, bug rates are climbing.
>
> A common rule of thumb is 20% of sprint capacity for tech debt. You could also use a cost-of-delay framework to compare the two.

**Problems**: Treats this as a static allocation decision. Ignores that tech debt compounds over time. Applies a generic ratio without understanding the phase. "20% rule of thumb" is a convention, not a strategy.

### After (cognitive protocol active)

> This is a compound-effect question, not a trade-off. Tech debt accumulates interest — each month of delay makes future development slower, which makes future features more expensive, which creates more pressure to take shortcuts, which creates more debt. It's a reinforcing loop.
>
> The answer depends on your phase:
>
> **Defensive phase (pre-product-market-fit)**: Speed of learning matters more than code quality. You may rewrite everything once you find PMF. Take the debt — but consciously, with a plan to pay it down once you know what you're building. Pseudo-patience about code quality here can kill the company.
>
> **Stalemate phase (PMF found, scaling)**: This is where debt becomes lethal. Every week of delay compounds. You're building the foundation that the next 5 years will run on. Invest heavily in paying down debt now — this is accumulation-phase work that looks boring but shifts the force ratio.
>
> **Offensive phase (rapid growth)**: You need both. But the debt must not slow your capture speed. If it does, debt reduction is an offensive enabler, not a tax.
>
> The 20% rule treats time as neutral. Time is not neutral here — it's amplifying whichever pattern you're in.

**Active shifts**: Compound awareness (Shift 1) — identified that debt interest compounds. Phase recognition (Shift 2) — different answer for each phase. Anti-pattern check — flagged the 20% convention as ignoring compound dynamics.

## Example 2: Business — Growth vs. Profitability

**User**: "Should we invest in growth or profitability?"

### Before (default mode)

> This depends on your stage and market conditions:
>
> **Invest in growth if**: you're in a winner-take-all market, your unit economics are healthy, you have access to capital, and competitors are also investing.
>
> **Focus on profitability if**: the market is cooling, fundraising is difficult, your burn rate is unsustainable, or you need to demonstrate financial discipline.
>
> Many companies sequence this: grow first to establish market position, then optimize for profitability once you've reached scale. The key metrics to watch are LTV:CAC ratio and months to payback.

**Problems**: Presents growth vs. profitability as a binary choice or simple sequence. Doesn't assess compound direction of either path. Ignores phase-transition dynamics (what happens when the growth phase ends?). "Grow then profit" is a convention that may not survive a phase transition.

### After (cognitive protocol active)

> First, the compound direction question: which investment gets stronger over time?
>
> Growth spending that builds durable advantages (network effects, data flywheels, switching costs, brand) compounds — every dollar invested makes the next dollar more effective. Growth spending that buys users through subsidies or promotions does not compound — it requires constant reinvestment and often attracts the wrong users.
>
> So the real question is not "growth vs. profitability" but "what kind of growth?"
>
> Now, the phase question:
>
> **Defensive phase (limited runway, uncertain economics)**: Profitability is survival. You cannot compound if you don't exist. This isn't "focusing on profitability" — it's buying time to reach stalemate phase.
>
> **Stalemate phase (sustainable economics, building position)**: Invest in compounding growth mechanisms. Not user acquisition at any cost, but infrastructure that makes future acquisition cheaper: product quality, data assets, ecosystem partnerships. This is 韬光养晦 — build strength while appearing disciplined.
>
> **Offensive phase (clear advantage, window of opportunity)**: Deploy for growth aggressively, because the accumulated advantages amplify every dollar. This is when the compound engine runs at full speed.
>
> Buffett's test: "Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre." If you stopped investing today and just maintained, would your business strengthen or weaken over 3 years? If weaken, your "growth" isn't compounding — it's renting.

**Active shifts**: Compound awareness (Shift 1) — distinguished compounding growth from non-compounding growth. Phase recognition (Shift 2) — different answer per phase. Sustainability check (Shift 5) — applied the Buffett test. Anti-pattern check — flagged "grow then profit" as linear extrapolation that ignores phase transitions.

## Example 3: Career — Higher Pay vs. Learning Opportunity

**User**: "Should I take the higher-paying job or the learning opportunity?"

### Before (default mode)

> This is a classic career decision. Consider:
>
> **Higher pay**: Financial security, ability to save/invest, recognition of your current value, may open doors through prestige.
>
> **Learning opportunity**: Skill development, career optionality, network building, potential for higher future earnings.
>
> If you're early in your career, most advice says prioritize learning. If you have financial obligations, the higher pay might be necessary. Try to quantify: how much more will you earn over 10 years with the new skills vs. the immediate salary difference?

**Problems**: Presents a static comparison. The "quantify over 10 years" suggestion sounds temporal but is actually linear extrapolation. Doesn't identify which option compounds. Doesn't consider phase.

### After (cognitive protocol active)

> Skills compound. Salary doesn't.
>
> A salary increase is a one-time level shift — you earn more now, but the increase itself doesn't generate further increases. Skills, relationships, and reputation compound — each unit makes acquiring the next unit easier and unlocks nonlinear opportunities.
>
> But watch out for pseudo-patience (anti-pattern #2). "I'm investing in learning" must be backed by specific, accumulating capabilities. If the "learning opportunity" is actually just lower pay for vaguely interesting work, that's not compounding — it's undervaluation.
>
> Phase check:
>
> **Defensive (financial stress, obligations)**: Take the money. You can't compound from a position of survival stress. Financial stability is the platform from which compound investments become possible.
>
> **Stalemate (stable, building)**: This is where the learning choice pays off most. You're in accumulation phase. The skills and network you build now are the force that tips the ratio in your favor for the offensive phase. The salary difference over 2-3 years is trivial compared to the career trajectory difference over 10.
>
> **Offensive (strong position, multiple options)**: You likely don't face this trade-off cleanly. If you do, optimize for the role that lets you deploy your accumulated skills at the highest leverage point.
>
> The key diagnostic: In 5 years, which version of you has more options? More options = more compounding surface area. That's your answer.

**Active shifts**: Compound awareness (Shift 1) — identified skills as compounding, salary as one-time. Phase recognition (Shift 2) — different answer per phase. Time-boundary extension (Shift 4) — reframed from "which pays more now" to "which creates more options in 5 years." Anti-pattern check — flagged pseudo-patience risk in the "learning" choice.

## Example 4: Investment — Stock Dropped 20%

**User**: "The stock dropped 20%, should I sell?"

### Before (default mode)

> A 20% drop is significant. Here are some considerations:
>
> **Sell if**: the fundamentals have changed, the thesis is broken, you need the money, the position is oversized for your portfolio.
>
> **Hold if**: the fundamentals are intact, you believe in the long-term thesis, you can tolerate further drops.
>
> **Buy more if**: nothing has changed except the price, and you'd buy at this price if you didn't already own it.
>
> It's important to separate emotion from analysis. Set a stop-loss if you need downside protection. Consider dollar-cost averaging out if you're uncertain.

**Problems**: Generic advice that doesn't engage with temporal dynamics. "Fundamentals intact" is the right question but doesn't go deeper. Stop-loss suggestion optimizes for short-term loss prevention, potentially sacrificing compound returns. No phase recognition.

### After (cognitive protocol active)

> A 20% price drop is a time-boundary problem (塞翁失马). Whether it's "bad" depends entirely on the time horizon and compound direction.
>
> First, the compound direction question: Is the underlying business in a position where time is its ally? Does it have durable competitive advantages that compound (brand, network effects, cost structure, ecosystem)? Or is it in a decaying position where time works against it (market shrinking, technology shifting, advantages eroding)?
>
> **If time is the business's friend**: The 20% drop is a gift. You're getting a compounding asset at a discount. The stock price is a short-term opinion; the business trajectory is a long-term fact. Buffett: "Time is the friend of the wonderful business." A wonderful business at 20% off is more wonderful, not less.
>
> **If time is the business's enemy**: The 20% drop is a signal you should have exited earlier. Sunk cost patience (anti-pattern #5) says "hold because I'm down." Temporal wisdom says "every day in a decaying position compounds the loss."
>
> Phase check for the market:
>
> **Defensive phase (broad market crash, recession fears)**: Good businesses get sold alongside bad ones. This is when compounding investors build positions — buying compound assets at defensive-phase prices. But ensure you have the runway (cash reserves) to survive the defensive phase.
>
> **Single-stock decline in stable market**: This is more diagnostic. Why did it drop? If the business's compound mechanism is intact and the market overreacted to noise, hold or add. If something structural broke, reassess.
>
> The fatal mistake: using a stop-loss or panic-selling a compounding asset during a defensive phase. You permanently exit a position that would have recovered and compounded. That's linear thinking (price went down = bad) defeating temporal wisdom (compound direction hasn't changed).

**Active shifts**: Time-boundary awareness (Shift 4) — "good or bad depends on time horizon." Compound direction (Shift 1) — the deciding factor is whether the business compounds, not whether the price dropped. Phase recognition (Shift 2) — broad crash vs. single-stock decline require different analysis. Anti-pattern check — flagged sunk cost patience as the wrong reason to hold, and stop-loss selling as short-termism applied to a compounding asset.
